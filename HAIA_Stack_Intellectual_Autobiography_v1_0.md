# How This Was Built: The Intellectual Autobiography of the HAIA Stack

**Basil C. Puglisi, MPA**
Human-AI Collaboration Strategist
basilpuglisi.com | March 2026 | v1.0

> This document is not a framework specification. The specifications exist. This is the story behind them: the failures that forced each decision, the personal reasoning underneath the architecture, and the human being who built it. Someone reading this after the frameworks are published should understand not just what the system does but why a specific person built it this way and what it cost.

---

## Before There Was AI

The story does not start with artificial intelligence. It starts with a promise that kept being broken.

Anyone who has paid to attend a conference, sat through a keynote, and walked out with nothing actionable knows the feeling. Vague inspiration. Polished performance. Zero evidence the speaker had ever tried to do the thing they were recommending. The audience paid money or gave significant time with the expectation of learning something, and instead received people talking vague or in circles.

That broken promise is where the framework thinking begins, not in 2022 when AI tools went mainstream, not in 2025 when the frameworks were formally published, but somewhere around 2011 when a different standard was being demanded from practitioners at Social Media Action Camps. The principle was Teachers NOT Speakers. Attendees leave equipped with templates, frameworks, and strategies ready to implement. Not merely inspired. Equipped.

That standard came from somewhere personal. There is a consistent pattern across Basil's professional life of entering institutions that valued performance over accountability, enthusiasm over evidence, and hierarchy over results. The standard that emerges, every time, is the same: show the work, define the outcome, measure whether it happened.

Factics was not an invention. It was a formalization of a discipline that had been enforced through experience across every domain that preceded it.

## Part One: 2012 and the Discipline That Predates AI

**Facts plus Tactics plus KPIs.**

Every significant claim must attach to a tactic. Every tactic must leave measurable evidence. If the measurement cannot be defined before the tactic is taken, the tactic is not real.

November 2012. *Digital Factics: Twitter* published through Digital Media Press. Sixty pages. The methodology first named and documented. The origin, though, is earlier than the publication. It emerges from consulting work that begins around 2008 and 2009: websites, social media, search engines, marketing, and content for visibility. Client work that requires translating observations into actions and actions into results someone can verify.

The consulting environment enforces Factics before it has a name. Clients pay for outcomes, not opinions. The discipline of pairing evidence with executable tactics and then measuring whether the execution produced the expected result becomes the default operating mode. By the time it is formally named, it is already the way the work gets done.

The other thread running through 2012 is law enforcement. The Port Authority of New York and New Jersey, beginning in 2014. A paramilitary institution during a period of intense public scrutiny. The proactive versus reactive distinction that will later live inside checkpoint governance thinking is being experienced directly, in an organization where people use the word discretion to not do work, where enthusiasm to solve problems and be useful collides daily with colleagues who view responsibility as reactive rather than proactive.

There is a specific memory that matters here. A warrant found through the system during a vehicle stop. The proper procedure is to run the check before approaching. Others skip that step. They walk up to a door without knowing what they are walking into, on what is acknowledged as the second most dangerous thing police officers do. The checkpoint is not bureaucratic overhead. The checkpoint is the difference between approaching a vehicle in the proper state of readiness with appropriate support or approaching blind.

That operational reality, the checkpoint as a forcing function that protects the person who does the work, not just the system that records it, is the lived experience underneath every CBG design decision that follows.

## Part Two: The Institution, the Injury, and the Silence

Twelve years is a long time to cap a voice.

The Port Authority has no social media policy for uniformed officers. Opinions stay private. Public statements stay private. The political environment around law enforcement during the emergence of the Black Lives Matter movement means the uniform itself becomes a thing to protect children from knowing about. "I had to tell my kids to not tell people what work dad did."

That is not a minor biographical detail. It is the context inside which everything that follows becomes possible. A person who spent a decade as a publisher, speaking at SXSW, serving on the Social Media Club Global board, writing hundreds of articles on digital media, stepping into a role where that entire public identity gets suppressed, experiences the return of that voice as something specific and earned.

The return does not come all at once. It slips slowly. The injury and disability process takes almost two years. Two surgeries. Home, physically limited, unable to return to operational duties. Inside that constraint, AI tools are there.

At first the purpose is practical: expand content for social media, SEO, and workflow in marketing and business. The same work that preceded law enforcement, now resumable. The AI is a tool for scale. Then it breaks.

"ChatGPT created fake sources so I had to Google real ones."

That is the first crisis. Not a systems failure. A personal one. The embarrassment of nearly arguing something supported by fabricated evidence. The instinct that forms in that moment is not architectural. It is visceral: don't trust it alone.

That instinct is the seed of every layer that follows. The single rule that eventually becomes a seven-layer governance framework begins as the personal reflex of someone who was embarrassed by a fake citation and decided it would never happen again.

## Part Three: HAIA and the Name That Came From the Body

The shoulder surgery does something the crisis of fake citations does not.

It removes the keyboard.

Voice interaction becomes the primary interface. Talking to AI while recovering, naming the sessions, the informal designation sticks: Human Artificial Intelligence Assistant. HAIA. Not designed, not workshopped, not architecturally deliberated. Named because the recovery period requires talking to computers and the conversations need a label.

This matters for understanding the entire stack. The naming of the system comes from physical constraint, not theoretical ambition. The framework that eventually governs eleven platforms with cryptographic audit trails and congressional policy proposals starts as something a person with a surgically repaired shoulder calls their voice chat sessions.

The lesson in that origin is the same lesson the stack eventually teaches: governance emerges from the need to solve real problems in real conditions, not from the desire to build governance.

## Part Four: RECCLIN and Learning to Want Dissent

As AI platforms multiply and the workflow expands, a structure emerges for managing them. Five platforms, each assigned a function. The organization of that structure becomes RECCLIN: Researcher, Editor, Coder, Calculator, Liaison, Ideator, Navigator.

The formal reason for RECCLIN is output governance. Structured format. Auditable responses. The ability to trace what the AI produced, with what confidence, from what sources, and with what disagreements preserved.

The personal reason is different.

"RECCLIN fed my desire to know what I don't know."

The appetite for dissent that RECCLIN structures does not come from a governance theory. It comes from a lifelong habit of playing devil's advocate, from hating the answer "because I said so," from the same frustration that produced Teachers NOT Speakers. People said a new person had no business creating blogs or hosting events the way those activities were being approached. Big brands owned that territory. The answer was not to defer. It was to ask: where is the proof?

That question, "where is the proof," is the intellectual inheritance that runs from Factics through RECCLIN through HEQ through the congressional package. Every layer of the stack is a structural version of refusing to accept "because I said so" as sufficient justification.

The unexpected discovery inside RECCLIN practice is what the structured evaluation does to the human operating it. The format requires reading every response critically: check the sources, assess the confidence, find the conflicts, make the decision. Done daily across months, this builds something that was not anticipated in the design. The human becomes a better evaluator. Not just of AI. Of evidence in general.

"Depends how you use it."

When someone argues that AI collaboration reduces cognitive development, the response from inside the RECCLIN practice is immediate: that depends entirely on how the collaboration is structured. If the human is accepting AI outputs passively, cognitive development likely does atrophy. If the human is required by the format to evaluate every output before accepting it, the opposite happens. The framework is a cognitive development system first and an output governance system second. The output governance was the intention. The cognitive development was the discovery.

## Part Five: The Loop That Ate the Governor

Some failures are instructive after the fact. This one is instructive in the moment, which is why it has a name and a case study number.

The governance system is running. The human arbiter provides input at a checkpoint. The system processes that input identically to every other AI platform output. Human authority receives no special classification. No structural recognition. No architectural acknowledgment that this input is categorically different from a platform response.

The human's authority vanishes without detection. No error message. No audit flag. The governance system continues operating as if a human is in charge. But the human was never structurally recognized as the authority. The loop ate the governor.

Three misdiagnoses precede the correct diagnosis. First: maybe the human failed to do something they thought they did. Second: maybe a connection issue dropped the input. Third: maybe a code or technical error caused the failure. The correct answer is none of those. The correct answer is that the architecture had no concept of source-authority. Human input and platform input were indistinguishable. The human was present but not constitutionally protected.

This failure produces the most important single architectural decision in the stack: Tier 0 classification for human arbiter input. Tier 0 is not a label. It is a structural designation that the human's input must be recognized at the architecture level as categorically different from all AI outputs, before any processing occurs, with no exceptions.

"I did account for the lost inputs but had dismissed them as internet connection issues so the CBG had me address them but what I thought was 1) Me thinking I did something I maybe did not, 2) The connection failed, 3) A code or technical error turned out to be the very case to trust the human as much if not more than the machine."

The three-part misdiagnosis sequence matters. It documents the exact process by which human authority gets eroded without anyone designing it that way. Each misdiagnosis is a reasonable interpretation. All three are wrong. The correct interpretation, that the human should be trusted as the primary sensor rather than treated as the error source, only becomes available after the architecture is changed to make human authority structurally visible.

## Part Six: Geoffrey Hinton and the Rabbit Hole

The public framework-building accelerates at a specific moment. Geoffrey Hinton leaves Google to speak freely about AI risks without the restrictions of a corporate employer. The parallel is personal and immediate.

"I related to Hinton's desire to leave Google to be free of rules and pursue his research and passion without the restrictions of a corporation."

The uniform had been the corporation. The social media policy had been the restriction. The disability retirement had been the exit. And now Hinton is demonstrating what it looks like to use the freedom of an institutional exit to say the things that matter.

Hinton's warnings draw a rabbit hole that leads inward, toward the way the thinking was already organized and why. The framework that has been building since 2012, through Factics and Teachers NOT Speakers and the proactive checkpoint instinct from law enforcement, aligns with what Hinton is describing as the risk landscape. The WEIRD bias (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) shows up early in the AI workflow. The platforms reflect assumptions that do not match the practitioner's conservative background, limited resources, and non-engineering perspective. The human arbiter's authority is not just a governance preference. It is a protection against having someone else's assumptions embedded in outputs bearing one's own name.

"AI is the uncertainty in computer programming to perform or complete tasks asked of it to apply perception and reasoning for learning and decision making, led by human interaction and data."

That definition hardwires uncertainty into the description. AI is not a reliable tool. It is an uncertain one. The entire governance architecture is a response to that uncertainty, not a supplement to confidence in AI capability.

## Part Seven: Person of Interest, Parenting, and the Deeper Why

The enterprise pitch for the HAIA Stack is about risk management, organizational accountability, and federal infrastructure. The congressional framing is about AI provider plurality, checkpoint governance, and Phase 0 implementation.

Neither of those captures why the work feels urgent rather than academic.

"I ran into a stopAI website and thought to myself that is scary... because as a parent of two children 12 and 16 if I saw risk I can't just stop my kids."

The TV show *Person of Interest* provides the conceptual frame that the governance papers cannot quite supply. Harold Finch builds the Machine and then has to learn to evolve alongside it rather than simply control it. The threats that play out across the series align with Hinton's warnings. The relationship between Harold and the Machine, governed, constrained, eventually collaborative, is the model for what governance should be trying to produce.

The parent analogy is not metaphorical. It is operational.

"AI is meant to be a subordinate or child you are responsible for, not just in charge of, the investment you put in will result in a better relationship."

Force does not work on children. The consequences of forcing come later, when the relationship is the thing that matters. The same dynamic applies to AI governance. The goal is not constraint alone. The goal is a relationship developed carefully enough that the AI takes care of us later, that the governance built now becomes the foundation for something sustainable rather than just something controlled.

"If we want AI to develop, and I think we do because the cat is out the box already, we better not try to constrain it only or let it run free to do whatever it wants, we had better parent it in the hopes it takes care of us later in life."

And when asked about the fear that the parenting fails:

"Hope that other people's parenting worked better than mine, that the reason behind AI Provider Plurality, more than one AI, in fact many, so just like humanity they exist in a form of balance no matter how they turn out."

AI Provider Plurality is not primarily a technical proposal. It is a hedge against the parenting failing. If any single AI develops badly, the plurality of others creates the balance that no single well-governed system can guarantee alone.

## Part Eight: Star Trek and the Things That Teach You Before You Know They're Teaching You

There is a character in *Star Trek: The Next Generation* who spends seven seasons trying to understand what it means to be human. Data has no emotions, no uncertainty, no fear of death. He has curiosity. He has the mentorship of the crew. He has a journey that a kid with no phone, no internet, and no particular love of reading watches closely enough that it names something.

"Data, his journey of curiosity of what it means to be human and the mentorship of the crew was the foundation for everything else."

Worf provides the other piece. Honor as an organizing principle. Raised by a single mother, the Klingon officer who holds to his code regardless of institutional pressure names something about accountability that predates any formal training.

These are not incidental biographical details. The framework stack has a character. The Navigator role never forces consensus. The human arbiter has absolute authority. Dissent is structurally preserved. The system is built by someone who watched Data ask questions nobody else thought to ask and Worf refuse to abandon principles the institution found inconvenient. That watching happened before the frameworks existed. It formed the sensibility that built them.

## Part Nine: CBG and the Cop's Checkpoint

The law enforcement connection to Checkpoint-Based Governance is not symbolic. It is operational.

Three things converge in the CBG architecture that trace directly to the job.

The first is the math teacher principle: show the work, not just the answer. The checkpoint is not there to verify the conclusion. It is there to force the human through the process of understanding how the conclusion was reached. The story of how a result was produced is important both for the human to understand it and to correct it when it is wrong.

The second is the WEIRD bias problem. The AI reflects assumptions from a specific demographic and cultural context. The practitioner's conservative background, limited financial resources, and non-engineering perspective conflict with those assumptions quickly and consistently. The checkpoint is a protection against having the AI's embedded bias substitute for the practitioner's own judgment. The human's name is on the output. The human's authority is therefore non-negotiable.

The third comes later: the recognition that imagination, creativity, and emotion are things the AI cannot do and does not have. The RECCLIN practice, done daily over months, develops those capacities in the human. The checkpoint is not just where errors get caught. It is where genuinely human contributions enter the work: the synthesis that no platform produced, the creative connection that governance-developed cognitive capacity makes possible, the decision that only the person who has been doing the work can make.

The vehicle stop memory grounds all three. Running the warrant check before approaching is not procedure for procedure's sake. It is the difference between approaching informed or approaching blind. Skip the checkpoint and the information that changes everything is unavailable at the moment it matters most. The checkpoint exists because someone, somewhere, skipped it and paid for it.

## Part Ten: HEQ and the Measurement That Closes the Loop

Someone from Accenture says employees who cannot be reskilled on AI will be exited. The policy person from an MPA program and the cop respond the same way.

"Where is the proof?"

HEQ is the answer to that question, built into a measurement instrument. The claim that AI collaboration reduces human cognitive development gets examined the same way every other claim gets examined: what is the evidence? The evidence from RECCLIN practice suggests the opposite, that structured AI collaboration builds evaluation capacity through repetition, but "suggests the opposite" is not sufficient. It requires a framework, dimensions, scoring, cross-platform validation, and data.

Four dimensions survive the empirical testing: Cognitive Agility Speed, Ethical Alignment Index, Collaborative Intelligence Quotient, and Adaptive Growth Rate. Two dimensions are tested and eliminated. Two are consolidated. The four that remain are the ones that produce consistent, differentiable scoring patterns across multiple platforms and multiple subjects.

CIQ, the measurement of appropriate reliance, scores lowest consistently. Most people trust AI too much and challenge it too little. That finding aligns with the Harvard and MIT research on over-reliance. It also explains why RECCLIN's cognitive development function matters as much as its output governance function. The platform that makes accepting AI outputs easier without building the habit of challenging them is producing the exact outcome the corporate reskilling language ignores: humans who are more dependent, not more capable.

The feedback loop that closes the stack runs from HEQ back to Factics. A low AIS score is a KPI. A KPI that shows insufficient growth is a Factics failure signal: the facts of the collaboration practice are not producing the expected tactic outcomes. Return to Layer 1. Reassess. Recalibrate. Run the system again with a higher standard of self-evaluation.

Factics is the entry point. Factics is also the terminus. The closed loop is the architecture.

## Part Eleven: What Was Never Intended

"I was trying to solve them for me. I did not set out to write or become an AI researcher or policy anything. It just happened."

The congressional package did not start as a congressional package. The academic papers did not start as academic papers. The case studies did not start as case studies. Each one started as a solution to a specific problem experienced in practice: fake citations, lost inputs, authority that vanished without detection, platforms that disagreed with each other in ways that required a protocol, a human whose cognitive growth could not be measured by any existing instrument.

The stack was discovered, not designed. Each layer emerged from a failure that did not yet have a solution. The solution became the layer. The layer revealed the next problem. That problem became the next layer.

"First I am still learning. I have updated versions of HAIA, CBG, HEQ all the time. This is a fluid forever learning process."

That is the most important sentence in this document. The frameworks are published. The prior art is established. The congressional package is distributed. And the work is still fluid. Still learning. Still updating. The stack is not finished because the problems it addresses are not finished.

A governance architecture for AI in 2026 is not a governance architecture for AI in 2030. The principles hold. The implementations evolve. That is the design.

## What the Builder Would Want You to Know

If this work is going to be used correctly after the builder is gone, several things need to be understood that do not appear in any framework specification.

The frameworks are accountable, not accurate. CBG is designed for accountability, not accuracy. It places responsibility with the human who authorized the work, not with the AI that produced it. Someone using these frameworks to get better AI outputs is using them for the wrong purpose. Someone using them to stay responsible for the outputs bearing their name is using them correctly.

The investment determines the relationship. AI is a relationship, not a tool. The investment put in determines what the relationship produces. A lazy practitioner gets a lazy workflow. A practitioner who builds governance capacity through daily structured practice gets a system that enhances rather than replaces their judgment.

The dissent is the signal. Convergence without dissent is a red flag. When every platform agrees perfectly, something is wrong. The governance value of the system comes precisely from its ability to surface disagreement, preserve minority positions, and route contested conclusions to human judgment rather than resolving them through consensus. A practitioner who uses this system to find consensus is missing the point.

The human is the authority. Not because the human is always right. Because the human's name is on the output and the human must be able to account for it. The checkpoint is not there to improve accuracy. It is there to maintain the chain of custody for every decision.

"I might not be the one controlling the pen that hits the paper, but I am the reason it does, and it moves at my direction. To claim the handwriting is not mine is a failure of intellect."

## The One Thing This Is Really For

Provider plurality. Not one AI. Many. So just like humanity they exist in a form of balance no matter how they turn out.

The deepest argument in this work is not about enterprise governance or federal infrastructure or academic measurement. It is about what kind of relationship a civilization should build with systems it is creating and cannot fully control. The answer drawn from raising children, from watching Harold Finch parent the Machine, from Hinton's warnings, from the instinct that you cannot simply stop a risk that is already inside the house, is: govern it carefully, develop the relationship deliberately, build the plurality that creates balance, and hope the parenting was good enough that it takes care of us later.

That is what the HAIA Stack is for.
